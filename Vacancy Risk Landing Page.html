The problem

Philadelphia has a vacancy problem. The records don't show all of it.

Vacant properties are one of the most visible signs of disinvestment in a neighborhood. They attract illegal dumping, reduce property values for surrounding owners, create fire hazards, and signal to residents that a block is being left behind. Philadelphia has a lot of them.

But the city's official vacancy count, compiled from Licenses & Inspections records, utility shutoffs, and 311 complaints, has a known gap. Many properties that are functionally vacant never make it into the administrative record. An owner may be paying water bills on a property no one lives in. A building may sit empty for years before a neighbor files a complaint. The data reflects enforcement history, not ground truth.

That gap matters for two reasons. First, L&I can't inspect what it doesn't know about. Second, community development organizations, housing courts, and city planners making decisions about where to invest resources are working from an incomplete picture.

This model was built to close part of that gap. By combining dozens of signals (311 complaint history, building permit activity, parcel characteristics) it produces a probability score for every residential parcel in the city. The higher the score, the more a property looks like other properties that turned out to be vacant.

The goal isn't to automate enforcement. It's to give the city a prioritized list of addresses worth a second look, grounded in data rather than chance or proximity to the last complaint.

Why records undercount?
Administrative vacancy data is a byproduct of enforcement, reflecting where inspectors have been, not where vacancies exist. Properties with absentee owners, paid-forward utilities, or no active neighbors are systematically underrepresented.
  • 34 Data features used to train the model, across 6 administrative source systems
  • 5 yrs Historical lookback window on utility, permit, and complaint data
  • 521 Census tracts used for spatial cross-validation — ensuring the model works citywide, not just where training data is dense
How it works

From raw administrative data to a ranked list of addresses.

1
Data assembly
Six city data systems — L&I, OPA, USPS, water utility, 311, and permits — are joined at the parcel level. Each of Philadelphia's ~520,000 residential parcels gets a row.
2
Feature engineering
Raw fields are transformed into 36+ predictive signals: utility usage trends, permit gaps, complaint frequency, tax delinquency status, structural age, and more.
3
Model training
A gradient-boosted classifier is trained on confirmed-vacant properties from L&I records. Spatial cross-validation across Philadelphia's districts prevents overfitting to dense-data areas.
4
Probability scoring
Every parcel receives a calibrated probability score (0–1). Scores are published to the dashboard and refreshed when new source data is available. High-risk parcels surface to the top.

About Philly Stat 360

Philly Stat 360 is the City of Philadelphia's performance management initiative. We track how city government is doing — across every department, in plain language — and publish the results for every resident to see.

This vacancy risk model is part of a broader effort to use data to make city services more proactive — finding problems before they become crises, and doing it fairly.
